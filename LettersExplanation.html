What is MBTI?

The Myers-Briggs Type Indicator (MBTI) is one of the most widely used personality frameworks in the world. It is designed to help individuals understand their preferences in how they perceive the world and make decisions. Developed by Katharine Cook Briggs and her daughter Isabel Briggs Myers, the MBTI is based on Carl Jung's theories of psychological types. By categorizing people into 16 personality types, it offers valuable insights into human behavior, relationships, and personal development.

How Does MBTI Work?

The MBTI divides personality into four dichotomies, or pairs of traits, that represent opposite preferences. These are:

  • Extraversion (E) vs. Introversion (I): How individuals direct their energy and attention—toward the external world or inwardly toward thoughts and ideas.
  • Sensing (S) vs. Intuition (N): How individuals prefer to gather information—focusing on details and facts versus patterns and possibilities.
  • Thinking (T) vs. Feeling (F): How individuals make decisions—based on logic and consistency versus values and emotions.
  • Judging (J) vs. Perceiving (P): How individuals approach life—favoring structure and planning versus flexibility and spontaneity.

What Can MBTI Be Used For?

MBTI is a versatile tool with applications in various aspects of life, including:

  • Self-Understanding: Gain insights into your strengths, weaknesses, and preferences to foster personal growth.
  • Career Guidance: Identify career paths that align with your personality traits and work style.
  • Team Collaboration: Enhance communication and teamwork by understanding different personality types in group settings.
  • Relationships: Improve interpersonal relationships by recognizing and appreciating the differences in others' approaches and preferences.

While MBTI is a powerful tool, it's important to note that it does not define an individual's full potential or predict future behavior. Instead, it serves as a starting point for deeper self-reflection and understanding.
